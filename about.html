Understanding Nuclear Power

Nuclear energy is generated by splitting atoms in a process called nuclear fission. This releases enormous amounts of heat, which is then used to produce electricity. Unlike fossil fuels, nuclear power produces minimal greenhouse gas emissions during operation.

Step 1: Nuclear Fission

Inside a nuclear reactor, uranium or plutonium fuel rods absorb neutrons. This causes the atoms to split, releasing heat and more neutrons. The chain reaction is carefully controlled to ensure safety and efficiency.

Step 2: Heat Transfer

The heat produced from fission is used to heat water. This creates steam, which is directed through pipes to drive turbines. The closed system ensures that radioactive materials remain safely contained.

Step 3: Electricity Generation

The steam spins large turbines connected to generators. As the turbines rotate, they produce electricity that is transmitted to homes, businesses, and industries through the power grid.

Step 4: Cooling

After passing through the turbines, the steam is cooled back into water using cooling towers or water from nearby rivers and oceans. The water is then recycled back into the system to be reheated.

Safety and Control

Nuclear reactors are designed with multiple safety systems. Control rods absorb excess neutrons to regulate the chain reaction, while containment structures prevent radiation from escaping. International standards ensure strict monitoring and safe operation.
